### Impossible Coordination
#Why the world has broken coordination and how to fix it

A stout gentleman with glasses and greying hair stood at the front of the classroom. When Dr. Bernard Amadei began his guest lecture, his subtle French accent grabbed the class's attention. He started with something along the lines of “You know you're in a bad situation when you're measuring how much life you have left in minutes instead of years”. Dr. Amadei -the founder of Engineers without Borders USA- has spent a lifetime organizing humanitarian collaboration in the world’s most chaotic countries. After his lecture, I jogged to catch him outside the classroom and asked “why can't these countries escape poverty?”. The engineer in me expected that electrical infrastructure was the culprit. The answer Dr. Amadei provided, surprised me - “corruption”. 
The misallocation of resources due to negligent information management is called a coordination failure- and corruption is the most extreme example. However, coordination failures are the normal operating state in nearly all organizations. 
The Root Cause
Problems existed, but they existed in peoples heads and inboxes- both of which were inaccessible to me. When the marketing team needs a construction update, the accounting team needs coordinates, or the operations team needs an engineering drawing- they rarely go to the construction intern first- yet in each of those real examples, I was the right person to call. Don Norman would call this an uncrossable Gulf of Execution: I had the capability to act but the work was structurally invisible to me. We need to be able to collaborate without personally knowing each other. Instead we coordinate through an organisational chart that was designed in the 1800’s for reductionalist, repeatable, factory work [3].
Stigmergy
An underappreciated coordination feat is the road. The meticulously designed traffic system allows millions of independent agents to coordinate peacefully and without any direct communication. The stoplight tells me when to move, the brake lights in front of me indicate I should slow down, the blinker warns me a car intends to change trajectory. In summary, the environment tells me the rules I abide by and what to do next. This is expert design and stigmergy. Stigmergy is an indirect coordination mechanism, by which traces in the environment (brake lights, stopsigns, etc.) stimulate the subsequent action (slow down, change lanes, etc.). Through stigmergy, coordination can occur without the need for centralized control, mutual awareness, or even communication. The modular nature of stigmergy makes it such that it can hypothetically scale to infinite complexity. This is the case with ant farms, which use stigmergic pheromone trails to productively coordinate the actions of billions of agents- imagine if people could do that. 
The Substrate
I propose “The Substrate”, a digital platform built for stigmergic collaboration. Unlike Outlook, Slack and Teams, an entire company may collaborate on the Substrate- with individuals of all ranks posting traces into a shared medium. The prototype is presented in figure 1, and represents a hypothetical company building a house. Color provides affordances that indicate which traces (tasks) are open, closed, active, and are locked until their prerequisites are met. Lines show dependencies between traces. When you click on a task, like frame structure, you enter a subsidiary flow chart that has the same structure as the larger whole, in this way the substrate is infinitely scaleable, just like stigmergy is.
